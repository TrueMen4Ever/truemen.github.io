True Men

This is a place where you can learn about true manhood.

A “true man” nurtures mind, body, and spirit, guided by virtues such as courage, generosity, and responsible leadership. True manhood emphasizes accountability and care for others, rather than entitlement or privilege—reflecting the principle that “men are protectors and maintainers of women” (Qur’an 4:34). Courage, generosity, and competent guidance are essential traits, as noted in prophetic teachings: “A man should not be stingy, cowardly, or incapable of leading” (Sunan Abu Dawud, Hadith 2850). Leadership, therefore, is less about authority and more about service—toward family, community, and society—rooted in ethical and spiritual cultivation.

Courage should be exercised in the pursuit of justice, generosity directed toward the well-being of others, and leadership tempered by humility (Al-Ghazali, Ihya’ Ulum al-Din). Contemporary scholarship echoes this holistic ideal: authentic manhood combines self-mastery with meaningful contribution to society (Connell, 2005). Ultimately, a true man is defined not by status or dominance, but by consistently embodying virtues that uplift both self and community.

The Filter of True Men:

  • Courage: Facing challenges, protecting the vulnerable, and standing for justice.
  • Generosity: Sharing resources, time, and knowledge for the benefit of others.
  • Responsible Leadership: Guiding with integrity, humility, and accountability.

Additional qualities such as self-mastery, compassion, and ethical integrity support and reinforce these core traits, shaping a holistic and authentic manhood.

Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ)

Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), regarded as the final messenger in Islam, is widely recognized as the seal of prophethood and a paragon of moral and spiritual excellence (Esposito, 2011). Born in Mecca in 570 CE, he was orphaned at a young age and raised under challenging social conditions in a society marked by tribal conflict and moral decline (Ibn Ishaq, 1955/1974). Even prior to the reception of revelation, he was known as Al-Amin, “the Trustworthy,” due to his reputation for honesty, integrity, and ethical conduct (Lings, 1983). At the age of 40, he received the first revelation, beginning a prophetic mission that he pursued despite significant social, economic, and personal hardships. He faced ridicule, persecution, and loss, yet remained steadfast in his commitment to monotheism and the ethical reform of society (Rahman, 1979).

Muhammad’s life exemplifies a balance of mercy, firmness, and ethical leadership. As a military leader, he demonstrated strategic acumen and courage, but his actions were consistently tempered by principles of justice and proportionality (Ibn Hisham, 1955/1997). In his domestic life, he exhibited compassion, humility, and attentiveness to his family, engaging in household duties and treating his wives with respect and equity (Ali, 2006). Socially, he maintained strong interpersonal bonds: he remembered the names of his companions, visited the sick, supported the vulnerable, and honored the elderly (Lings, 1983). As a political and community leader, he governed with transparency, forgiveness, and service, prioritizing the welfare of the ummah over personal power (Esposito, 2011). His spiritual devotion was characterized by nightly prayers, supplication, and intercession for the broader community, reflecting his deep empathy and moral responsibility.

The prophetic model demonstrates moderation, ethical consistency, and spiritual guidance, emphasizing practical and timeless principles applicable across contexts. Muhammad’s example provides a framework for moral excellence, social justice, and spiritual cultivation, serving as a paradigm for individuals striving for integrity, humility, and resilience in both personal and societal spheres (Rahman, 1979; Lings, 1983). His legacy continues to inform contemporary discussions on ethics, leadership, and religious practice, highlighting the enduring relevance of his teachings.

Jesus (Isa عليه السلام)

Jesus (ʿĪsā), recognized as one of the most revered prophets in Islam, is understood as a divinely appointed messenger who conveyed God’s guidance to humanity. According to Islamic sources, he was born miraculously to the Virgin Maryam without a father, a sign of God’s omnipotence and mercy (Qur’an 3:45–47; 19:16–22). From birth, his life was marked by divine favor: as an infant, he spoke in defense of his mother, proclaiming himself as a devoted servant of God (Esposito, 2011). Throughout his prophetic mission, he performed numerous miracles—including healing the blind and lepers, raising the dead, and giving life to clay birds—by God’s permission, all as signs intended to guide people toward monotheism and moral rectitude (Qur’an 5:110).

Jesus’ life exemplified asceticism, humility, and moral integrity. He led a life detached from worldly wealth, political power, and material possessions, traveling from town to town to preach love, repentance, and submission to God’s will (Rahman, 1979). He emphasized ethical conduct, compassion, and sincere worship, confronting corrupt authorities and advocating for justice and humility. His actions—including serving others, showing empathy for the afflicted, and forgiving those who opposed him—reflect an integration of spiritual devotion and ethical practice (Nasr, 2003; Esposito, 2011).

In Islamic theology, ʿĪsā is not considered the son of God but a mighty messenger and a pure creation of God’s word and spirit (Qur’an 4:171). He was not crucified but was raised up by God and will return before the end of time to restore justice and truth (Qur’an 4:157–159). His legacy embodies spiritual surrender, moral courage, and humility, providing a model for ethical living that prioritizes divine obedience over worldly dominance and pride. Through his example, ʿĪsā demonstrates that true greatness is found in submission to God and in service to humanity (Lings, 1983).

Moses (Musa عليه السلام)

Moses (Mūsā in Arabic) is among the most frequently mentioned prophets in the Qur’an, and his life exemplifies trials, spiritual transformation, and unwavering trust in God (Esposito, 2011). Born during a period when Pharaoh ordered the execution of all newborn Israelite boys, Mūsā’s early life was marked by danger. By divine intervention, he was safely raised in Pharaoh’s palace, receiving an upbringing that later equipped him to confront tyranny and lead his people (Ibn Kathir, 2003). Following an accidental killing, Mūsā fled into exile, where he spent years in solitude and reflection, culminating in his divine encounter at the burning bush—a pivotal moment that signaled the beginning of his prophetic mission (Qur’an 20:9–24).

Despite his stutter and humble background as a shepherd, Mūsā returned to Pharaoh armed not with armies but with divine signs, commanding, “Let my people go” (Qur’an 7:104–105). His courage was grounded in absolute reliance on God rather than personal ambition. He endured rejection by his own community, humiliation from Pharaoh’s court, and carried out numerous miracles by God’s permission, including the parting of the sea, which facilitated the Israelites’ escape and the drowning of Pharaoh (Qur’an 26:63–66). These events highlight Mūsā’s role as both a spiritual and political leader, exemplifying steadfastness, faith, and divine guidance in the face of oppression.

Mūsā’s mission continued long after these dramatic events. He spent decades guiding a people often resistant to instruction, demonstrating patience, persistence, and moral leadership. His prolonged fasts, including the 40 nights on Mount Sinai to receive revelation, and his intense yearning to witness God, reflect his deep spiritual commitment (Qur’an 7:142–143). Mūsā’s life illustrates that true leadership emphasizes service over dominance, persistence over perfection, and the conquest of internal doubt alongside external challenges. His example provides enduring lessons in faith, resilience, and ethical responsibility (Nasr, 2003; Rahman, 1979).

Abraham (Ibrahim عليه السلام)

Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham in the Judeo-Christian tradition) is one of the most revered patriarchs in Islam, celebrated for his unwavering faith, rational inquiry, and total submission to God’s will (Esposito, 2011). From an early age, he challenged the idolatry and false practices of his people, even confronting his own father in defense of monotheism (Qur’an 6:74–79). His intellectual and spiritual inquiry led him to reject the worship of celestial bodies—the stars, moon, and sun—ultimately affirming exclusive devotion to the Creator of all existence (Nasr, 2003). Ibrahim’s narrative exemplifies courage, reasoning, and moral steadfastness in the pursuit of divine truth.

Throughout his life, Ibrahim faced extreme trials that tested his faith and obedience. When cast into a blazing fire for rejecting idolatry, God protected him, rendering the fire harmless (Qur’an 21:69). He undertook arduous migrations at God’s command, leaving behind comfort and security. Notably, he obeyed divine instructions to leave his wife Hajar and infant son Ismail in the barren desert of Makkah, and he demonstrated complete submission when commanded in a vision to sacrifice his son, an act halted by divine mercy (Qur’an 37:100–111). These episodes reflect not blind faith, but conscious surrender rooted in trust, devotion, and moral conviction (Rahman, 1979).

Ibrahim’s title, Khalīlullāh—Friend of God—reflects his exceptional closeness to the Divine. His legacy endures in Islamic ritual, including the rites of Hajj, the establishment of the Kaaba, and the call to prayer, serving as a model for pious conduct and moral integrity (Esposito, 2011). His life demonstrates that true faith integrates reason, obedience, and sacrifice, providing a timeless paradigm for spiritual, ethical, and personal development.

Noah (Nuh عليه السلام)

Prophet Nūḥ (Noah) is recognized in the Qur’an as a model of resilience, perseverance, and unwavering commitment to divine truth (Esposito, 2011). Sent to a people entrenched in idolatry and moral corruption, he preached the message of monotheism for an extraordinary period of 950 years, calling both publicly and privately for repentance and return to God (Qur’an 29:14; 71:1–28). Despite his persistent efforts, only a few accepted his guidance. He endured ridicule, mockery, and social ostracism, yet remained steadfast in his mission, driven by sincere concern for his community rather than personal recognition (Nasr, 2003).

Nūḥ’s life exemplifies patient obedience and moral fortitude. Following God’s command, he constructed the ark, laboring amid the laughter and derision of his people (Qur’an 11:36–40). His absolute trust in God was rewarded when the flood came, delivering divine justice to the corrupt while preserving the righteous. His deep emotional connection is illustrated in his grief over his son, who disbelieved and perished, highlighting the personal cost of prophetic responsibility and the tension between mission and familial attachment (Rahman, 1979).

The legacy of Nūḥ underscores the principles of perseverance, ethical steadfastness, and moral courage. His story teaches that truth is not validated by popularity, and sincere commitment is measured by sacrifice and dedication to principle. For those striving to uphold values in the face of societal opposition, Nūḥ provides a paradigm of patient endurance, faithful action, and unwavering adherence to divine guidance (Esposito, 2011; Nasr, 2003).

Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal

Imam Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal (780–855 CE) stands as a central figure in Islamic scholarship, renowned for his steadfast commitment to orthodoxy and ethical integrity during a period of significant political and theological turmoil (Robinson, 2001). Living under the Abbasid Caliphate, he became a prominent symbol of intellectual resistance during the Mihna (inquisition), when scholars were pressured to endorse the doctrine that the Qur’an was created. Imam Aḥmad’s refusal, despite imprisonment, beating, and torture, upheld the traditional Sunni belief in the Qur’an’s uncreated, eternal nature, demonstrating his unwavering devotion to God over personal safety (Hallaq, 1984).

Beyond his theological courage, Imam Aḥmad made monumental contributions to hadith studies, memorizing vast numbers of traditions and compiling the Musnad, one of the most comprehensive collections of prophetic narrations (Brown, 2009). His life combined rigorous scholarship with ethical practice: he taught discreetly under oppressive conditions, supported the needy, forgave his detractors, and engaged in prolonged nightly worship. His humility, patience, and moral courage exemplify the integration of knowledge and ethical action in Islamic leadership, showing that true strength lies in adherence to divine principles rather than worldly power or recognition (Robinson, 2001; Hallaq, 1984).

Imam Aḥmad’s legacy continues to inspire Islamic scholarship and moral fortitude, demonstrating that scholarly excellence and ethical resilience are mutually reinforcing. His example underscores the importance of maintaining integrity, moral courage, and submission to God in the face of social, political, or intellectual pressure.

  • Hallaq, W. B. (1984). Imam al-Shafi’i: Scholar and Statesman. Oxford University Press.

Imam Shafi'i

Imam al-Shāfi‘ī (767–820 CE) is recognized as a foundational figure in Islamic jurisprudence, celebrated for his intellectual rigor, eloquence, and systematic approach to legal theory. He synthesized principles from the Qur’an, Sunnah, consensus (ijmā‘), and analogical reasoning (qiyās), laying the groundwork for the development of the Shāfi‘ī school of law (Hallaq, 2005). His sharp memory, mastery of debate, and poetic eloquence contributed to his reputation as both a scholar and a jurist of exceptional clarity, earning respect from contemporaries and critics alike (Makdisi, 1981).

Despite extensive travels and studies under prominent scholars of his time, Imam al-Shāfi‘ī maintained humility and devotion to learning. He emphasized adherence to the Sunnah and the Qur’an while remaining open to reasoned interpretation, stressing that the pursuit of truth should take precedence over personal gain or recognition (Hallaq, 2005). His guiding principle, "If I speak the truth, follow me; if my companions speak the truth, follow them," reflects his commitment to intellectual honesty, communal consultation, and ethical scholarship (Makdisi, 1981). Through his work, Imam al-Shāfi‘ī shaped the methodology of Islamic law, leaving a lasting legacy that continues to influence legal and ethical thought in Muslim societies.

References:

  • Hallaq, W. B. (2005). The Origins and Evolution of Islamic Law. Cambridge University Press.
  • Makdisi, G. (1981). The Rise of Colleges: Institutions of Learning in Islam and the West. Edinburgh University Press.
Imam Abu Hanifa

Imam Abū Ḥanīfa (699–767 CE) is recognized as a pioneering figure in Islamic jurisprudence, notable for integrating rational inquiry with strict adherence to divine revelation. As both a successful merchant and an independent scholar, he supported students of knowledge and developed the foundations of the Ḥanafī school, which became the most widely followed legal tradition in the Muslim world (Hallaq, 2005). His legal acumen was matched by his ethical integrity; he refused to endorse political appointments he considered unjust, resulting in imprisonment and corporal punishment, demonstrating his unwavering commitment to principle over personal safety (Makdisi, 1981).

Imam Abū Ḥanīfa taught thousands of students, emphasizing critical reasoning, reflection, and principled action. He advocated a jurisprudence that combined intellect with piety, asserting that Islamic law is not merely a codification of rules but a framework for lived ethics encompassing mercy, justice, and moral responsibility (Hallaq, 2005). Through his teachings, Abū Ḥanīfa established not only a legal school but also a culture of ethical conscience, shaping generations of scholars and the broader development of Islamic legal thought.

References:

  • Hallaq, W. B. (2005). The Origins and Evolution of Islamic Law. Cambridge University Press.
  • Makdisi, G. (1981). The Rise of Colleges: Institutions of Learning in Islam and the West. Edinburgh University Press.
Imam Malik

Imam Mālik ibn Anas (711–795 CE) is recognized as one of the foremost jurists of early Islam, particularly associated with the city of Madinah, the Prophet’s (ﷺ) city of guidance. He is renowned for his reverence for Hadith, meticulous methodology, and ethical rigor, culminating in the compilation of the Muwatta, the earliest systematic collection of Islamic law and prophetic tradition (Hallaq, 2005). Imam Mālik maintained independence from political authority, refusing to allow rulers to manipulate religious rulings for political ends, an integrity that at times subjected him to public flogging and social pressure (Makdisi, 1981).

Imam Mālik exemplified the integration of personal piety, scholarly discipline, and ethical conduct. He emphasized that true knowledge is not merely the accumulation of reports but a divine illumination placed in the heart by God, and he refused to transmit Hadith unless in a state of ritual purity (wudu) (Brown, 2009). His composure, careful speech, and moral consistency illustrate a life in which scholarly excellence was inseparable from spiritual and ethical integrity, providing a model of calm, rooted, and radiant scholarship for subsequent generations of Muslim jurists and students.

Khabib Nurmagomedov

Khabib Nurmagomedov (b. 1988) is widely recognized not only as a dominant figure in mixed martial arts (MMA) but also as a model of discipline, humility, and faith in professional sports (Sherdog, 2020). Raised in the mountainous region of Dagestan, Russia, Khabib’s upbringing was shaped by a rigorous regimen of physical training and moral education under the guidance of his father, Abdulmanap Nurmagomedov, a prominent wrestling coach. His approach combined technical mastery with personal development, emphasizing self-discipline and ethical conduct alongside athletic achievement (O’Reilly, 2021).

Throughout his professional career, Khabib remained undefeated, a rare accomplishment in the high-stakes world of MMA. His success was accompanied by a consistent demonstration of personal values: humility in victory, restraint in conflict, and respect toward opponents, coaches, and family members. He has frequently highlighted the role of faith, parental respect, and moral responsibility in his life, articulating a philosophy in which true strength is inseparable from ethical conduct and spiritual commitment (Sherdog, 2020; O’Reilly, 2021).

Khabib’s example illustrates a broader conception of masculinity and leadership in sports, one that privileges self-control, loyalty, and ethical integrity over aggression or notoriety. In a cultural environment often dominated by spectacle and ego, he demonstrates that personal excellence and adherence to moral and spiritual principles can coexist with global fame and competitive dominance.

Malcolm X

Malcolm X (1925–1965) emerged as a transformative figure in the struggle for racial justice and civil rights in the United States. His early life, marked by familial disruption, systemic oppression, and incarceration, shaped a profound understanding of social injustice and personal resilience (Marable, 2011). As a spokesperson for the Nation of Islam, he articulated the dignity of Black Americans and the necessity of confronting racial inequities with principled courage, emphasizing self-respect, accountability, and empowerment (Glaude, 2016).

Malcolm’s intellectual and spiritual evolution deepened following his pilgrimage (Hajj) to Mecca, where he experienced the universality of Islam and the ethical imperatives of justice, mercy, and unity (X, 1965). This experience catalyzed his transition from a militant activist to a more inclusive da‘i, advocating human rights and emphasizing ethical action grounded in the Qur’an and Sunnah. His well-known statement, "I am for truth, no matter who tells it. I’m for justice, no matter who it’s for or against," encapsulates his commitment to universal principles over partisan allegiance (X, 1965; Marable, 2011).

Malcolm X’s life exemplifies a dynamic conception of masculinity, characterized by personal growth, ethical integrity, and the courage to confront both internal and societal challenges. His enduring legacy underscores the importance of moral conviction, intellectual rigor, and spiritual alignment in fostering leadership and social responsibility (Glaude, 2016).

Muhammad Ali

Muhammad Ali (1942–2016) was not only a preeminent figure in professional boxing but also a prominent cultural, social, and religious icon. Beyond his athletic prowess, Ali embodied the integration of faith, ethics, and activism in public life (Hauser, 1991). He famously refused induction into the U.S. military during the Vietnam War, citing religious convictions and conscientious objection, a decision that led to legal challenges and the temporary stripping of his boxing titles (Roberts, 2004).

Ali’s commitment to social justice extended beyond legal resistance. He actively advocated for civil rights, racial equality, and community empowerment, demonstrating moral courage in the face of widespread public criticism and financial penalties (Hauser, 1991; Roberts, 2004). His adherence to Islam and principled stance informed both his public persona and personal conduct, reinforcing the interplay between spiritual discipline and ethical leadership. Even as Parkinson’s disease gradually affected his physical abilities, Ali’s dedication to charitable work, prayer, and mentorship reflected a deepening of spiritual and moral resilience (Hauser, 1991).

Muhammad Ali’s life exemplifies a conception of masculinity that merges physical courage with ethical integrity, spiritual devotion, and social responsibility. His legacy underscores that true greatness lies not merely in athletic achievement, but in principled action, moral leadership, and steadfast commitment to one’s beliefs (Roberts, 2004).

Alexander the Great

Alexander III of Macedon (356–323 BCE), commonly known as Alexander the Great, remains one of history’s most influential military leaders and strategists. Ascending the throne of Macedon at the age of 20, he embarked on a decade-long campaign that created one of the largest empires of the ancient world, stretching from Greece to Egypt and into northwest India (Green, 1991). His military genius lay in combining innovative tactics, disciplined organization, and psychological insight to achieve battlefield supremacy.

Beyond his martial accomplishments, Alexander sought to integrate diverse cultures within his empire, promoting Hellenistic ideals while adopting elements of local governance and customs (Cartledge, 2004). He encouraged cultural exchange, founded cities such as Alexandria in Egypt, and patronized arts and sciences, leaving a lasting legacy on the intellectual and social fabric of the regions he conquered. His leadership style combined charisma, strategic vision, and personal bravery, although historical accounts also note instances of ruthlessness and authoritarianism (Green, 1991).

Alexander’s life exemplifies the interplay between ambition, leadership, and cultural influence. While his conquests demonstrate the potential of disciplined strategy and audacity, his efforts to blend cultures underscore the importance of vision and adaptability in leadership. His legacy continues to be studied for its military, political, and cultural significance across multiple disciplines (Cartledge, 2004).

References:

  • Cartledge, P. (2004). Alexander the Great: The Hunt for a New Past. Overlook Press.
  • Green, P. (1991). Alexander of Macedon, 356–323 B.C.: A Historical Biography. University of California Press.
Maryam (Mary عليها السلام)

Maryam (Mary), the mother of Isa (Jesus), occupies a central position in Islamic theology as a paradigm of piety, devotion, and divine selection. The Qur’an emphasizes her unique status among women, describing her as chosen and purified by Allah, and highlights her unwavering faith in the face of extraordinary trials (Qur’an 3:42–47; 19:16–26) (Safi, 2018). From an early age, Maryam dedicated herself to worship and spiritual discipline within the sanctuary, reflecting a life centered on devotion and moral integrity.

Her narrative encompasses the challenges of miraculous conception, societal scrutiny, and personal solitude. When informed of her role in giving birth to Isa, Maryam responded with steadfast submission and trust in God, exemplifying resilience and ethical fortitude (Qur’an 19:23–26) (Nasr, 2003). The Qur’an portrays her calmness and dignity in addressing her community, allowing Isa to speak on her behalf, highlighting her exceptional spiritual strength and reliance on divine guidance (Safi, 2018).

Maryam’s life exemplifies the Islamic conception of honor and strength, demonstrating that true greatness derives from moral and spiritual excellence rather than worldly status. Her story remains a timeless model for women and men alike, illustrating the virtues of patience, purity, and absolute trust in God (Nasr, 2003).
